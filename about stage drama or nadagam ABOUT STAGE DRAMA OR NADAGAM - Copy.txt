when did stage drama or nadagam stop ?

Stage drama or “nadagam” never completely stopped in Tamil culture — but its dominance declined gradually, mainly between the 1970s and 1990s because of cinema and later television. Even today, there are still active drama troupes and sabha performances in Chennai and other places.

A rough timeline:

1900s–1950s: Nadagam was one of the main entertainment forms. Famous playwrights and performers like Pammal Sambandha Mudaliar helped modern Tamil theatre grow.
1950s–1970s: Tamil cinema exploded. Many stage actors, writers, and comedians moved into films because cinema reached larger audiences and paid better.
1980s: Television became common. Tamil TV dramas started growing after Doordarshan serials and later private channels.
1990s onward: Sun TV serial culture and cable TV heavily reduced theatre audiences. Live drama became more niche, mostly supported by sabhas, festivals, or political/cultural groups.

The decline happened for several reasons:

Cinema was cheaper and more glamorous.
TV serials brought drama directly into homes.
Stage productions became expensive.
Younger audiences shifted to films and TV.
Many top theatre talents migrated to cinema.

Still, theatre never died. Groups continue performing works inspired by legends like Komal Swaminathan, whose play Thanneer Thanneer was hugely successful on stage in the 1980s.

Even recently, articles note that Tamil amateur theatre still survives in Chennai through sabhas and around 30 active troupes, though with smaller audiences than before.

what about sound films in 1930's and color films in the 1950's ?

Yes — sound films in the 1930s and color films in the 1950s were two huge turning points that weakened traditional stage drama (nadagam), but in different ways.

1. Sound films (“talkies”) in the 1930s — the biggest blow

Before sound, silent cinema could not fully replace live theatre because people still wanted:

dialogue,
songs,
comedy,
emotional acting,
live music.

But once talkies arrived — especially after the first Tamil talkie Kalidas in 1931 — cinema suddenly offered everything stage drama had, plus:

multiple locations,
close-up acting,
recorded songs,
lower ticket prices,
repeat screenings.

Many early Tamil films were literally filmed versions of famous stage plays. For example:

Pathi Bhakthi came from a successful theatre play,
Chintamani was also adapted from a famous stage drama.

The important thing is:

early Tamil cinema did not destroy theatre immediately — it absorbed it.

Most actors, singers, comedians, and writers came directly from drama companies.

In fact, scholars note that Indian sound cinema “struck a mortal blow” to many folk and live theatre traditions because audiences preferred musical talkies.

So the 1930s were the beginning of the decline of nadagam’s mass popularity.

2. Color films in the 1950s — accelerated the decline

By the 1950s, cinema became visually spectacular:

color,
grand sets,
dance sequences,
outdoor shooting,
star culture.

Tamil cinema’s first full-length color film was usually identified as Alibabavum 40 Thirudargalum.

Now audiences could see:

palaces,
forests,
battles,
fantasy worlds,
elaborate costumes

in ways impossible on a theatre stage.

At the same time:

stars like M. G. Ramachandran and Sivaji Ganesan became massive cinema icons,
studios expanded,
theatres (cinema halls) spread across towns.

This reduced commercial stage drama further. By then, live drama survived mainly through:

touring drama troupes,
political theatre,
comedy dramas,
sabha culture.
So which mattered more?
Sound films in the 1930s = the real turning point.
They replaced the core function of live drama.
Color films in the 1950s = made cinema even more attractive and commercially dominant.

Then television in the 1980s–90s pushed nadagam further into a niche form.

So Tamil theatre didn’t suddenly “stop” at one moment. It declined in waves:

1930s talkies → first major decline
1950s color + star cinema → faster decline
1980s TV serials → mass audience collapse
